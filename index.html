The Language of the Cloth

Patterns woven into meaning

Every motif on this kimono carries centuries of meaning. Read the cloth, and you read a poem.

鶴

Tsuru — Crane

Symbol of longevity and faithfulness. A pair of cranes mate for life and are said to live a thousand years.

青海波

Seigaiha — Waves

Endless waves represent peace and good fortune flowing forward without end.

桜

Sakura — Cherry blossom

Beauty in transience. Worn in spring, it whispers of life's brief, perfect moments.

麻の葉

Asanoha — Hemp leaf

Strength and protection. Traditionally worn by infants to grant rapid, healthy growth.

1987 — Origins

A small dyehouse on a narrow lane

The atelier was founded by Tanaka Hideko in the spring of 1987 — a single room above a tea merchant's shop in northern Kyoto. She dyed silk for nine local kimono houses while raising two daughters in the same building. The fire never went out.

Her granddaughter, Tanaka Sumire — who paints the cranes you see on our furisode today — joined the atelier in 2009 after returning from a textile residency in Lyon. She is the third generation of women to dye in this room.
