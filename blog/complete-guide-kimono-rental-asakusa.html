The Complete Guide to Kimono Rental in Asakusa, Tokyo

Everything you need to know before your first kimono day — pricing, what's included, and how to plan your visit around Sensoji.

Kimono Rental AnnJournal6 min read

If there's one place in Tokyo to wear a kimono, it's Asakusa. The neighborhood's narrow streets, wooden shopfronts, and the five-story pagoda of Sensoji Temple make it feel like a living backdrop — which is exactly why so many visitors choose to spend at least one afternoon here dressed in kimono rather than everyday clothes.

This guide covers the basics: what a kimono rental actually includes, how much it costs, and how to plan your visit so you get the most out of it.

What "kimono rental" actually means

Renting a kimono is different from buying one. You choose a style from a shop's collection, get dressed by a trained staff member (this part is called kitsuke, and it takes some skill — the layers, the sash, the fit all matter), and wear the outfit for the day before returning it before closing time. No sewing knowledge, no guesswork, no commitment.

At Kimono Rental Ann in Asakusa, the whole process — choosing, dressing, and finishing touches — usually takes under an hour, and walk-ins are welcome, no reservation required.

What's included

A rental isn't just the outer robe. A full kimono outfit includes the kimono itself, the obi (sash), undergarments, and accessories like a small bag and sandals. Most shops, Ann included, also offer hair styling as part of the experience, since a kimono looks best with hair pulled up and away from the collar.

How much does it cost?

Pricing depends on the style of kimono you choose. As a general range in Asakusa:

Yukata — from ¥4,500/day Komon — from ¥8,000/day Houmongi — from ¥12,000/day Furisode — from ¥18,000/day

We break this down in more detail in our pricing guide.

Where to go: Asakusa specifically

Asakusa isn't just convenient — it's the right look for a kimono photo. Sensoji Temple, the Kaminarimon gate, and the surrounding streets have the traditional architecture that makes kimono photos feel natural rather than staged. Ann's shop is a short walk from Sensoji, so you can be dressed and walking toward the temple within the hour.

A simple first-timer's plan

  1. Walk in (or reserve ahead if you prefer a set time)
  2. Choose your kimono and color
  3. Get dressed and styled — about 30–45 minutes
  4. Walk to Sensoji and the surrounding streets for photos
  5. Return the kimono by closing time — no need to rush, most shops give you the full day

That's really all there is to it. If you want more detail on any one step — picking the right style, what to expect during dressing, or where to take photos — the rest of this journal covers each in depth.
